What Is Supervised Machine Learning?

Learning from labeled examples — you give the model inputs AND the correct answers, and it learns the pattern.

Classification

Predicting a category

  • Will this patient be readmitted? (Yes/No)
  • Is this tumor malignant? (Benign/Malignant)
  • What disease does this patient have? (Multi-class)

Regression

Predicting a number

  • How long will this patient stay? (Days)
  • What will the treatment cost? (Dollars)
  • What is the risk score? (0–100)

Week 2

Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA)

Before you model, you must understand. EDA reveals what the data actually looks like.

What to Look For

  • Missing values — why are they missing?
  • Class imbalance — 95% healthy, 5% sick?
  • Outliers — data entry errors or real cases?
  • Correlations — which features relate?

Healthcare-Specific Traps

  • Survivorship bias — only patients who lived
  • Label noise — misdiagnoses in training data
  • Temporal leakage — using future info
  • Population bias — who was studied?
Week 2

Feature Engineering

The art of telling the model what to pay attention to. Domain knowledge is your advantage here.

Raw Data

  • Age, weight, blood pressure
  • Lab results, medication list
  • Visit dates, procedure codes

Engineered Features

  • BMI (from height + weight)
  • Days since last visit
  • Number of medications (polypharmacy)
  • Comorbidity index (combined risk)

Your decision: Which features should the model use? A clinician knows BMI matters more than raw weight. Encode that knowledge.

Week 2

Classification Models

Logistic Regression

The simplest classifier. Outputs a probability (0–100%). Highly interpretable — you can explain exactly why a patient scored high. Start here.

Decision Trees

A flowchart of yes/no questions. Easy to explain to clinicians: "If blood pressure > 140 AND age > 60, then high risk." But can overfit.

Random Forests

Many decision trees voting together. More accurate than a single tree, less prone to overfitting. Harder to explain, but feature importance still visible.

Week 2

Regression Models

When you need to predict a continuous number, not a category.

Linear Regression

Finds the best straight line through the data. Predicts: length of hospital stay, treatment cost, recovery time.

Ridge & Lasso

Linear regression with guardrails. Prevents overfitting when you have many features. Lasso can auto-select relevant features.

Gradient Boosting

Builds models in sequence, each fixing errors of the last. XGBoost, LightGBM — often the most accurate for structured data.

Healthcare Uses

Predict ICU length of stay, insurance claim amounts, patient risk scores, readmission probability (as a score).

Week 2

Ensemble Methods

Why combining models beats single models. The "wisdom of crowds" for machine learning.

Bagging

Train many models on random subsets of data, then average their predictions. Random Forest does this. Reduces overfitting.

Boosting

Train models sequentially, each focusing on the mistakes of the previous one. XGBoost, LightGBM. Often the most accurate.

Stacking

Use different model types and let a meta-model combine their predictions. Logistic regression + tree + SVM, blended by a final learner.

Trade-off: Ensembles are more accurate but harder to explain. In healthcare, explainability often wins.

Key Decision: Feature Selection & Bias

Which patient features should your model use? This is an ethical decision, not just a technical one.

Useful Features

  • Clinical vitals, lab results
  • Medical history, comorbidities
  • Medication adherence
  • Prior visit patterns

Dangerous Features

  • Race or ethnicity as predictor
  • Zip code (proxy for income/race)
  • Insurance type (proxy for wealth)
  • Language spoken (access barrier)

Real case: Optum's algorithm used healthcare spending as a proxy for health need — systematically deprioritized Black patients who had less access to care. Feature choice IS bias choice.

Week 2

Key Decision: Threshold Setting

Your model outputs a probability. You choose where to draw the line.

Low Threshold (e.g., 30%)

  • Flag more patients as at-risk
  • Catch almost all true positives
  • Many false alarms (alert fatigue)
  • Higher cost, fewer missed cases

High Threshold (e.g., 80%)

  • Flag only highest-risk patients
  • Very few false alarms
  • Miss some true positives
  • Lower cost, some missed cases

The question: Is it worse to miss a sick patient or to overload clinicians with false alarms? This is a business AND ethical decision.

Week 2

Key Decision: Interpretability vs. Accuracy

High Interpretability — Logistic Regression
Medium — Decision Trees, Rules-Based
Lower — Random Forest, XGBoost
Low Interpretability — Deep Neural Networks

In Healthcare

  • Clinicians need to know why
  • Regulators require explainability
  • "The model said so" is not enough
  • SHAP values can help explain complex models

Your decision: Can you sacrifice 2% accuracy for a model a doctor can actually trust and act on?

Week 2

Modern AI: LLMs in Healthcare

Large Language Models are transforming healthcare — but they complement, not replace, classical ML.

LLM Strengths

  • Clinical note summarization
  • Patient communication drafts
  • Medical literature search
  • Coding and billing assistance

Where Classical ML Wins

  • Structured data prediction (vitals, labs)
  • Real-time risk scoring at scale
  • Regulatory approval pathways
  • Auditable, deterministic outputs

The winning combination: Classical ML for prediction, LLMs for communication and summarization.

Week 2

Responsible AI in High-Stakes Domains

Fairness

Does the model perform equally well across demographic groups? Test for disparate impact before deployment.

Transparency

Can you explain how the model reaches its recommendation? Clinicians and patients deserve to understand.

Safety

What happens when the model is wrong? Fail-safes, human-in-the-loop, and graceful degradation are non-negotiable.

Healthcare is a "cannot undo" domain. A wrong loan decision costs money. A wrong clinical decision costs a life. Your product must reflect this.
