Week 4

What Is Optimization?

Different from prediction. Prediction asks "what will happen?" Optimization asks "what should we do?"

Predictive AI (Weeks 2–3)

  • Will this patient be readmitted?
  • Will this loan default?
  • What will demand be next month?
  • Output: a prediction

Prescriptive AI (This Week)

  • Which route minimizes fuel cost?
  • How should we allocate inventory?
  • What production schedule is optimal?
  • Output: a recommended action

Optimization = finding the best solution under constraints. You have limited trucks, limited time, limited budget — what's the best you can do?

Week 4

Linear Programming

Resource allocation under constraints. The mathematical foundation of optimization.

Three Components

  • Objective: What to maximize/minimize (cost, time, profit)
  • Variables: What you can control (routes, quantities, schedules)
  • Constraints: What you can't change (capacity, budget, deadlines)

Example: Warehouse Allocation

  • Objective: Minimize total shipping cost
  • Variables: How much to ship from each warehouse
  • Constraints: Each warehouse has limited stock; each store has minimum demand

Your decision: Define the objective and constraints. The solver finds the optimal solution. You judge whether the solution makes business sense.

Week 4

Constraint Optimization

When the real world gets complicated. Not everything is a straight line.

Scheduling

Assign workers to shifts, machines to jobs, vehicles to routes — all with overlapping constraints (availability, skills, labor laws).

Routing

Find the cheapest path visiting all delivery points. The "Travelling Salesman Problem" — one of the most studied optimization problems in history.

Capacity Planning

How many warehouses? Where to put them? How much inventory in each? Balance service levels against storage and transport costs.

Week 4

Genetic & Meta-heuristic Algorithms

When the solution space is too large to search exhaustively. Inspired by natural evolution.

How Genetic Algorithms Work

  • Start with random solutions (population)
  • Score them (fitness function)
  • Combine the best ones (crossover)
  • Add random changes (mutation)
  • Repeat until good enough

When to Use

  • Too many combinations to try all
  • No exact mathematical solution
  • "Good enough" is acceptable
  • Multi-objective optimization
  • Examples: route planning, scheduling

Trade-off: Genetic algorithms find very good solutions but not guaranteed optimal ones. In practice, 98% optimal delivered in 10 seconds beats 100% optimal computed in 10 hours.

Experimentation Sandboxing — MLflow

Track every experiment. Compare every model. Reproduce any result.

What MLflow Tracks

  • Parameters: what settings were used
  • Metrics: how the model performed
  • Artifacts: the model file itself
  • Code version: which code produced it

Why It Matters

  • "Which model is in production?" — answered
  • "Can we reproduce this result?" — yes
  • "Is the new model better?" — compare side-by-side
  • Audit trail for governance

Your decision: MLflow is part of your product's operational infrastructure. COC sets it up; you define what to track and when to promote a model to production.

AI Verify — Model Transparency & Governance

Singapore's framework for testing and certifying AI systems before deployment.

What AI Verify Tests

  • Transparency: Can you explain the model?
  • Fairness: Does it treat groups equally?
  • Robustness: Does it handle edge cases?
  • Safety: What happens when it fails?

Why It Matters for Supply Chain

  • Optimization affects workers (scheduling)
  • Route decisions impact communities
  • Inventory decisions affect access to goods
  • Automated decisions need human oversight

Your decision: Before deploying an optimization model, run it through AI Verify's testing framework. Document what it does, how it decides, and where humans override.

Week 4

Key Decision: Hard vs. Soft Constraints

Not all constraints are equal. Some are unbreakable. Others are preferences.

Hard Constraints

  • Vehicle capacity (physics)
  • Driver hours (labor law)
  • Temperature requirements (cold chain)
  • Regulatory compliance

Cannot be violated. Ever.

Soft Constraints

  • Preferred delivery windows
  • Driver route preferences
  • Cost targets (desirable but flexible)
  • Service level agreements

Can be bent. At a cost.

Your decision: Classify every constraint as hard or soft. Getting this wrong means either infeasible solutions or missed optimization opportunities.

Week 4

Key Decision: Objective Function Design

What you optimize for defines what you get. Choose carefully.

Minimize Cost

Cheapest routes, fewest trucks. Risk: slower delivery, worse customer experience, overworked drivers.

Minimize Time

Fastest delivery. Risk: higher cost, more fuel, more vehicles needed.

Maximize Sustainability

Minimize carbon footprint. Risk: slower, possibly more expensive. But increasingly required by ESG mandates.

The real answer is usually multi-objective: Minimize cost WHILE meeting delivery windows AND staying under carbon targets. You assign the weights.

Week 4

Key Decision: When to Retrain

Detecting model drift — when the world changes and your model hasn't.

Types of Drift

  • Data drift: input patterns change (new products, new suppliers)
  • Concept drift: the relationship changes (demand patterns shift post-COVID)
  • Performance drift: accuracy declines over time

Retraining Strategies

  • Scheduled: retrain weekly/monthly
  • Triggered: retrain when performance drops below threshold
  • Continuous: always learning from new data

Your decision: How often should the model retrain? What performance threshold triggers retraining? Who approves the new model before it goes live?

Week 4

Key Decision: Governance Before Deployment

An optimization model that's deployed without governance is a liability.

Gate 4: Human Approval — Final sign-off before production
Gate 3: AI Verify Testing — Transparency, fairness, robustness checks
Gate 2: Shadow Deployment — Run alongside existing system, compare results
Gate 1: Backtesting — Test on historical data, validate against known outcomes

Your decision: Define what "good enough" means at each gate. What metrics must the model hit before it's trusted with real supply chain decisions?

Modern AI: Agents & Digital Twins

The frontier of supply chain AI goes beyond optimization to autonomous action.

AI Agents for Supply Chain

  • Autonomous reordering when stock is low
  • Dynamic rerouting on disruptions
  • Supplier negotiation bots
  • Real-time demand response

Digital Twins

  • Virtual replica of your supply chain
  • Simulate scenarios before acting
  • "What if port X closes for 2 weeks?"
  • Test optimization changes risk-free

The combination: Digital twin simulates. Optimization recommends. AI agent executes. Human sets the boundaries and monitors.
