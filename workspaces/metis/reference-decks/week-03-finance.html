Week 3

Model Evaluation Deep Dive

"How good is the model?" has many answers. The right metric depends on the business problem.

Accuracy

What % of predictions are correct? Misleading when classes are imbalanced — a model that says "no fraud" 99% of the time is 99% accurate but useless.

Precision

Of all flagged items, how many were truly positive? High precision = few false alarms. Critical when flagging is expensive (freezing accounts).

Recall

Of all actual positives, how many did we catch? High recall = few missed cases. Critical when missing something is costly (fraud, defaults).

F1 Score

The balance between precision and recall. Use when you need both: catch fraud without harassing legitimate customers.

Week 3

The Confusion Matrix

Every prediction falls into one of four boxes. Understanding this grid is the key to choosing the right metric.

Predicted: Fraud Predicted: Legitimate
Actually Fraud True Positive — Caught it! False Negative — Missed fraud
Actually Legitimate False Positive — False alarm True Negative — Correct

In fraud detection: False negatives (missed fraud) cost money. False positives (blocking legitimate customers) cost trust. You decide the balance.

Week 3

AUC-ROC — The Model's Report Card

The ROC curve shows how well your model separates positives from negatives across all possible thresholds.

How to Read It

  • AUC = 1.0 — Perfect separation
  • AUC = 0.9 — Excellent model
  • AUC = 0.7 — Acceptable
  • AUC = 0.5 — Random coin flip

Business Translation

  • AUC tells you overall discrimination power
  • Does NOT tell you the best threshold
  • A high AUC model can still be poorly calibrated
  • Compare models, then tune the threshold

Your decision: AUC picks the best model. Then YOU set the threshold based on business costs.

Hyperparameter Tuning

Fine-tuning the model's settings — like adjusting the knobs on an amplifier to get the best sound.

Grid Search

Try every combination of settings. Thorough but slow. Good when you have few parameters to tune.

Random Search

Randomly sample combinations. Surprisingly effective and much faster. Often finds good settings with fewer tries.

Bayesian Optimization

Learns from previous attempts to make smarter guesses. The most efficient approach. COC handles this for you.

Your decision: COC tunes the model. You decide: is the improvement worth the added complexity?

Week 3

Key Decision: Which Metric?

Precision vs. recall in lending has real dollar costs.

Optimize for Precision

  • Fewer false alarms
  • Only flag truly risky applicants
  • More defaults slip through
  • Cost: bad loans that default

Optimize for Recall

  • Catch all risky applicants
  • Reject more borderline cases
  • Turn away good customers
  • Cost: lost revenue from rejections

Calculate it: If the average default costs $50K and the average rejected good loan earns $5K in interest — the math says optimize for recall. But customer relationships matter too.

Week 3

Key Decision: Fairness Audit

Does your model discriminate? In finance, this is both an ethical imperative and a regulatory requirement.

Check For

  • Different approval rates by gender, age, race
  • Proxy variables that encode bias
  • Disparate impact in model outputs
  • Training data representativeness

Regulatory Context

  • MAS: FEAT Principles (Singapore)
  • EU AI Act: High-risk AI classification
  • US: Fair lending laws (ECOA, FHA)
  • Explainability is a compliance requirement

Your decision: Run fairness checks on your model's output. If approval rates differ significantly by demographic group, you need to understand why — before deployment.

Week 3

Key Decision: Dashboard Design

Information vs. overload. A dashboard that shows everything helps nobody.

Good Dashboard

  • 3–5 key metrics visible at once
  • Clear hierarchy: summary then detail
  • Actionable: "What should I do?"
  • Progressive disclosure: drill down

Bad Dashboard

  • 20 charts on one screen
  • No clear takeaway
  • Informational: "Interesting, but so what?"
  • Requires training to understand

The test: Can a new user look at your dashboard for 30 seconds and know what to do next? If not, redesign.

Week 3

Key Decision: Threshold Economics

The profit-maximizing threshold is not always the fairness-maximizing threshold.

Profit-Maximizing

  • Set threshold where marginal revenue = marginal cost of default
  • May systematically exclude certain groups
  • Optimizes for the company

Fairness-Maximizing

  • Equalize acceptance rates across groups
  • May reduce short-term profit
  • Builds trust and meets regulatory requirements

Your decision: Where on the profit-fairness spectrum does your product sit? This is a strategic choice that defines your brand.

AI Governance Frameworks

Financial AI is heavily regulated. Know the frameworks before you build.

Singapore AI Verify

IMDA's AI governance testing framework. Tests for transparency, fairness, robustness, and accountability. Voluntary but industry-standard.

MAS FEAT Principles

Fairness, Ethics, Accountability, Transparency. Specific to financial institutions in Singapore. Your product must align.

EU AI Act

Risk-based classification. Credit scoring is "high-risk" — requires conformity assessment, documentation, and human oversight.
