What Is Unsupervised ML?

Supervised (Weeks 2–3)

You give it the answers

  • Labeled data: "this is fraud / not fraud"
  • Model learns to predict labels
  • Classification, regression

Unsupervised (This Week)

No labels — find hidden patterns

  • No "right answer" to learn from
  • Model discovers structure in data
  • Clustering, dimensionality reduction

Key insight: Supervised ML answers questions you already know to ask. Unsupervised ML reveals questions you didn't know existed.

Week 5

Clustering — Grouping the Unknown

Give the algorithm data points. It finds natural groups.

K-Means

You pick K groups. Algorithm assigns every point to the nearest center. Fast, simple, works well on spherical clusters.

DBSCAN

Finds clusters of any shape. Automatically detects outliers. No need to specify number of clusters upfront.

Hierarchical

Builds a tree of clusters. Useful when you need nested segments (e.g., "premium" splits into "luxury" and "aspirational").

Think of it as: Scattered dots on a page → colored groups. Each color is a segment with shared characteristics.

Week 5

How Many Clusters Are Real?

The algorithm will always find K clusters if you ask for K. The question is: how many are meaningful?

Statistical Methods

  • Elbow plot: graph error vs. K, look for the "bend"
  • Silhouette score: how well-separated are clusters?
  • Gap statistic: compare to random data

Business Logic Matters More

  • Can your marketing team act on 12 segments? Probably not.
  • Do the clusters tell a story you recognize?
  • Are they stable over time?
  • 3–7 segments is usually actionable
Week 5

Dimensionality Reduction

Your customer data has 100+ features. Humans can visualize 2–3. How do you bridge that gap?

PCA (Principal Component Analysis)

Finds the directions of maximum variance. Collapses correlated features into "components." Good for preprocessing before clustering.

t-SNE / UMAP

Visualization tools. Maps high-dimensional data to 2D while preserving local structure. Great for seeing if clusters exist.

Your decision: Use PCA to simplify before modeling. Use t-SNE/UMAP to visualize and validate. They serve different purposes.

Week 5

Recommender Systems

The engine behind "You might also like..." — worth billions to Amazon, Netflix, Spotify.

Collaborative Filtering

"People like you bought..."

  • Uses behavior of similar users
  • No need to understand the product
  • Needs lots of user interaction data

Content-Based Filtering

"Because you liked this product..."

  • Uses product attributes and features
  • Works for niche products
  • Needs good product metadata

Best practice: Hybrid systems combine both. Netflix uses collaborative + content + contextual signals.

Week 5

The Cold Start Problem

Recommendations need data. What happens when you have none?

New User

No purchase history, no browsing data. Solutions: ask preferences during onboarding, use demographics, show popular items, leverage content-based approach.

New Product

No ratings or interactions yet. Solutions: use product attributes (content-based), editorial curation, A/B test with small traffic, leverage similar products.

Business impact: Cold start directly affects new customer conversion and new product launch success. Your strategy here is a product decision, not a technical one.

What Is RAG?

Retrieval-Augmented Generation

LLMs are powerful but they don't know your products, policies, or prices. RAG solves this.

Without RAG

  • LLM guesses or hallucinates
  • Generic answers, no specifics
  • "I don't have information about your product catalog"

With RAG

  • LLM searches your knowledge base first
  • Answers grounded in real data
  • "The Nike Air Max 90 is $189, available in sizes 7–12"

Week 5

Decision: How Many Segments?

The algorithm gives you clusters. You decide which are real and actionable.

Too Few (2–3)

Easy to act on, but you miss nuance. "Big spenders" and "everyone else" isn't useful for personalization.

Too Many (15+)

Statistically perfect, operationally impossible. Your marketing team can't run 15 different campaigns.

The right answer: As many as your organization can meaningfully act on. Usually 4–7. Each segment should suggest a different action.

Week 5

Decision: What Goes Into RAG?

Your knowledge base defines what the AI can and cannot answer.

Include

  • Product catalog & descriptions
  • Pricing and availability
  • Return/shipping policies
  • FAQ and common questions
  • Size guides, compatibility info

Think Carefully

  • Customer reviews (helpful but noisy)
  • Competitor comparisons (legal risk?)
  • Internal memos (data leakage risk)
  • Historical pricing (do you want AI sharing old prices?)

Rule of thumb: If a well-trained sales associate should know it, put it in the knowledge base. If they shouldn't share it, don't.

Week 5

Decision: Personalization vs. PDPA

Singapore's Personal Data Protection Act sets clear boundaries. Better personalization needs more data. More data needs more compliance.

What You Can Do

  • Aggregate behavior (anonymous segments)
  • Consent-based personalization
  • On-device processing (no data leaves phone)
  • Pseudonymized analytics

Red Lines

  • Tracking without consent
  • Cross-platform data merging without notice
  • Inferring sensitive attributes (health, religion)
  • Selling personal data to third parties
Week 5

Modern AI: LLM Grounding

RAG is one form of grounding — connecting LLMs to real-world data. Here's the broader landscape:

RAG

Search a knowledge base before answering. Best for factual queries about your domain.

Tool Use

LLM calls APIs: check inventory, calculate shipping, process returns. Actions, not just answers.

Conversational AI

Multi-turn dialog with memory. Remembers context: "the shoes you asked about earlier are now on sale."

The trend: AI shopping assistants are replacing traditional search. By 2027, Gartner predicts 25% of e-commerce interactions will be conversational.
